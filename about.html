Who Is This Guy

TLDR: I got some brain differences. I was a bit of an issue in elementary school, then kicked some ass in high school. I went to a good college and did fine. I played a sport. Made pizzas for a year or so. Now I connect the biggest regions in innovation with a focus on health. I am reading some books and thinking some thoughts. Tell me I am wrong and why I am wrong.

Here's the gist: I come from a family of healers. My dad spent 32 years healing people in emergency rooms. My mom spent just as long healing the community around her. My sister is training to heal people too. I don't want to miss out or break the trend.

I grew up learning about life and healing from friends and family just like everyone else. I went to school and couldn't pick what to learn and didn't read or focus too good. I did well socially and athletically, and had a pretty impressive detention debt lined up for most of middle school. I did not get to go outside during recess much those years which is a great way to manage the symptoms of ADHD. All work and no play made Brett a dull boy.

I locked in during high school and connected with my class, athletic teams, and club members. I cared deeply about my teams and communities and pushed myself to be my best for them and tried to elevate everyone else in them. If you told me I was doing that at the time I would have gotten really embarrassed and told you "nuh-uh," and not studied even harder for the Mandarin quiz that was about to kick my butt. Same anti-serious energy on the field–I wore capes before and after rivalry games. Regardless, I was awarded California's Small School Baseball Player of the Year, Athlete of the Year, and delivered the commencement address during graduation. I wanted to be a part of excellent teams, but did not want to be seen as excellent in them. I was the perfect little communist.

When I got to college, I was fortunate enough to continue playing the sport I loved, but I had to pick which knowledge to pursue. Naturally, I picked everything and chose Politics, Philosophy, and Economics. I did not know it at the time, but I think I chose that major because I knew there was something sick about the world I was being pushed into after college and I wanted to understand the nature of it beyond just its symptoms. Maybe I did not want to put on a lab coat, but I did want to pick up a scalpel and see what was going on in there.

After that, I spent a year or so absolutely honing my craft at Blaze Pizza. I was on the oven and whipping up the best pizzas you could get comfortably under $20. At one point, during Meatball Mania May, a month-long promotion where you could add whole meatballs to your pizza order, I really hit my stride. I was the only one doing it in the whole store, and by the end of the month we were in the top five stores nationally for gross meatball output. It was easily one of the best bits I have ever been a part of, never failed to make me smile or make the customer uncomfortable. But all good things must come to an end, and my time at Blaze had an expiration date if I was to go on to heal things greater than lunchtime tummy rumbles.

That is when I left home, moved to San Francisco, and joined the Israel Economic Mission. It put me in a position to learn all about the business behind my dad's career. Finally, I could sell meatballs that actually helped people. But in all seriousness, I sit at the intersection between life-saving people and life-saving technology and get to interact with both. After a year and a half I am now the Head of Health Ventures for the organization. I am young, but that's when all the experts say you heal the best.

Now, I am embarking on a new journey of learning. I am reading books written in the context of healing while talking to every type of professional healer I can find.

Starting with Paul Auster's Mr. Vertigo to understand the dynamic nature of healing as well as the trust and submission required to heal physically and spiritually. John Carreyrou's Bad Blood and Clayton Christensen's The Innovator's Dilemma are next on my list to provide a framework and examples for evaluating the business of innovation within health. Atul Gawande's Being Mortal and Siddhartha Mukherjee's The Emperor of All Maladies will help me wrestle with the moral and existential question of what it means to heal and the personal and timeless battle it is to live. Lastly, I am tackling Paul Starr's The Social Transformation of American Medicine, T.R. Reid's The Healing of America, and Rick McIntyre's The Rise of Wolf 8, to compare and contrast how we care for each other in society at its most simple and most complex. I am happy to add book recs so please shoot some my way. May as well go whole hog on this one, the subject matter is life and death after all. I am also listening to podcasts and watching youtube. Sounds like amateur hour, I know, but tell that to Khan of the so-named Academy fame. Also, Ben and David over at Acquired do some quality research and storytelling.

I am doing this so people can tell me that I don't know what I am talking about. Hopefully, at that point, they will tell me why and I can learn a thing or two. And maybe they can, too. ∎

Tell me I'm wrong.
